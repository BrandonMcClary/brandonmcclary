How Coby White Changed Charlotte’s Second Unit

Coby White was not brought in to dominate the ball for 35 minutes a night. He was brought in to stabilize Charlotte’s bench, and so far he has done exactly that.

By Brandon McClary 2026 Hornets

When the Charlotte Hornets acquired Coby White just before the trade deadline, the whole league understood what the implications were. After a very inefficient and underwhelming start to the season from Tre Mann, White was brought in to take the second unit to another level.

During his time with the Bulls, White established himself as a true bucket and a scoring threat from all areas of the floor. He was often the main creator offensively, and over the past couple seasons could reasonably be considered their number one option. The two years prior to this season, he averaged over 33 minutes per game, which makes his current role even more impressive.

Coby White averages as a Charlotte Hornet
20.0 MPG · 15.7 PPG · 3.3 RPG · 3.3 APG · 45.6 FG% · 35.6 3P% · 85.7 FT%

That 20 minutes per game is a major adjustment, but White has embraced the role completely. He has seamlessly plugged into the rotation and understands how Charlotte plays. The Hornets do not rely on one clear number one option, but instead a group of scorers that take turns carrying the offense on any given night.

That said, White has had his fair share of takeover moments. In a four-game stretch from March 17 to March 24, he scored 27 points twice and averaged 22.5 points overall. More importantly, it is not just the scoring. It is the control he brings. The pace settles when he is on the floor, possessions feel more intentional, and the second unit actually has structure instead of just surviving minutes.

A real stabilizer off the bench

What once looked like a bench unit lacking direction now has a clear engine. The Hornets are 11–5 when White plays and 4–0 when he scores over 20, which does not feel like a coincidence. His presence alone has raised the floor of that group.

Coby White (#0) with now former teammte Josh Giddey (#3)

Not everything came easy, though. Charlotte gave up Collin Sexton in the deal, a fan favorite and impactful player in his own right. While Sexton has found success in Chicago, it is hard to argue that Charlotte’s bench would have the same identity or confidence without White leading it right now.

At the end of the day, this move was not about finding another star. It was about fixing a weakness. The Hornets needed stability off the bench, someone who could create, settle things down, and still go get a bucket when needed.

So far, White has done exactly that, and the results are starting to reflect it.
