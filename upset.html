The Hornets Might Be More Dangerous Than Their Seed Suggests

Charlotte may still be sitting in the play-in picture, but the way this team has played in 2026 says they are a much tougher matchup than a normal lower seed.

By Brandon McClary March 31, 2026 Hornets

The 2026 Charlotte Hornets have lit a fire in the city that we haven’t seen since the days of Kemba Walker. Since the turn of the new year, the team has gone an impressive 28–14 (.667), and to put that into perspective, the franchise’s best full-season winning percentage came back in 1996–97 when they finished 54–28 (.659).

This isn’t just a hot stretch. It’s close to historic for this franchise.

As of March 31, the Hornets sit in the 10th seed in the Eastern Conference. With a tough final stretch ahead, it would not be surprising if they stay right there. That would place them in the lower half of the play-in tournament, meaning they would need to win two straight games just to secure a playoff spot.

Not ideal.

But maybe not as bad as it sounds.

If Charlotte were to fight through the play-in and grab the 8th seed, they would likely face the Detroit Pistons, who have all but locked up one of the top spots in the East.

The matchup might be closer than it looks

On paper, that sounds like a rough draw. But the matchups tell a more interesting story.

The Hornets and Pistons have already faced off twice this season, with a third matchup coming on April 10 in what will be both teams’ second-to-last regular season game. The first meeting was ugly, with Charlotte getting blown out 112–86 in a game that never really felt close.

The second meeting changed the tone completely. Charlotte kept it tight all night and turned it into a physical, emotional game that felt much more like a playoff atmosphere than a regular season matchup.

LaMelo Ball (left) being guarded by Kevin Huerter (right)

The moment everyone remembers is the bench-clearing brawl that led to suspensions for Jalen Duren, Isaiah Stewart, Miles Bridges, and Moussa Diabate. But beyond the chaos, the bigger takeaway was simple.

Charlotte was not backing down.

For a team that has spent years searching for identity, that game felt different. The energy, the physicality, and the edge all stood out. It looked like a group that was finally comfortable playing meaningful basketball with pressure attached to it.

Detroit still escaped with a narrow six-point win, but it did not feel like some huge gap separated the two teams that night.

The upset seems all too perfect.

Now here is where things get even more interesting. The Pistons were fully healthy in that matchup. The Hornets were still trying to figure things out.

And now Detroit’s star Cade Cunningham is sidelined for the remainder of the regular season and potentially into the playoffs.

That changes a lot.

Without Cunningham, Detroit loses its primary playmaker, late-game creator, and the engine that drives the offense. Suddenly, what looked like a possible first-round walkover starts to feel a lot more competitive.

And it is no secret that the Hornets are playing with real confidence right now. They have already shown they can hang with strong teams, and maybe more importantly, they are playing with nothing to lose.

That is exactly the kind of team nobody wants to see in a play-in game or a first-round series.

Are they favorites? No.

But are they capable of making things uncomfortable and maybe even pulling off an upset?

Absolutely.

And if that April 10 matchup carries the same tension as the last meeting, it might end up feeling like a preview for something bigger.

For this Hornets team, this is only the beginning.
